Like Nothing You've Seen Before

The Moon Viewer is a tool for teaching phases of the moon and related topics. It is the first and only device that makes the students an interactive part of the Earth-Sun-Moon system. By stepping inside the model, students learn to transition between seeing the moon’s appearance from a polar view, to an earth bound view. This transition is necessary to form a true conceptual understanding of why we observe phases of the moon.

Polar View

Typical student view of the Earth-Sun-Moon system. Students observe that the Moon is always half lit and half dark.

Earth Bound View

Student uses the Moon Viewer to become part of the model, observing the Earth Sun Moon system from the Earth Perspective.

Phases of the Moon

Teacher and Student Demonstration

Using the model and a student volunteer, demonstrate the polar view and explain why the moon is normally illuminated half light and half dark. Then, have a student step into the ring and raise it to eye level with the “new moon” facing a light source (windows). Student is now in “earthbound” view. Have the student slowly turn their head counterclockwise and carefully observe each moon on the ring. What do you see? Record image on paper. “Why doesn’t the moon always appear half lit and half dark to us from the Earth?” What causes us to observe phases of the moon?

Moon Phases Small Group "Cookbook" Lab

Same as above except each group has a Moon Viewer.

Moon Phases Small Group Inquiry Based Lab

Present the students with the Moon Viewer moons detached from the ring and ask to them formulate a theory by arranging the moons in a manner that answers the following questions: “Why is the moon normally half-lighted and half-dark?” “Why doesn’t the moon always appear to us half lit and half dark throughout the month?” “Why do we see phases of the moon?” Turn them loose and watch the power of this inquiry based approach.

Solar and Lunar Eclipses

Begin by reviewing phases of the moon by placing a moon at different positions on the ring and asking them what phase of the moon is shown. Move the sun (light source) in different positions to help them conceptualize how moving the position of the sun doesn’t change the relationship between the sun, earth and moon.

Use a student volunteer inside the Moon Viewer with two moons. Designate a light source as the sun. Place a moon at new phase and one at full phase on the ring. Remind them that the student’s eyes represent an earthbound observer.

Demonstrate and discuss the different theories offered by students for the following questions. “What phase of the moon would be required for a solar eclipse to occur? For a lunar eclipse to occur? Why doesn’t an eclipse of the sun and moon occur every month?

If none stumble on the correct reasoning, demonstrate the five degree tilt in the lunar orbit and the conditions necessary to create a solar and lunar eclipse.

Repeat above using multiple Moon Viewers and small groups.

Annular and Total Solar Eclipses

Demonstrate the difference between an annular and total solar eclipse by placing a moon on the ring in new phase, and moving the ring closer and further away from a student standing inside the ring and asking them what happens to the “apparent diameter” of the moon. “How does a change in apparent diameter relate to annular and total solar eclipses? What type of orbit would cause this to occur?” Repeat above using multiple Moon Viewers and small groups.

Tides

Using a classroom globe, Moon Viewer and double sided tape, have students stick small index cards labeled “High” and “Low” on the globe to represent where each type of tide would be occurring for different moon placements on the ring. Setup several stations with varying moon phases and repeat the above. Define “Spring” and “Neap” tides. What cause the differences between them? If desired, this could lead into a discussion on Newton’s Universal Law of Gravity.

Elliptical Orbits

Using the flexability of the PVC ring, demonstrate the shape of an elliptical orbit by slightly compressing two opposite sides of the the Moon Viewer closer together. Show varying degrees of eccentricity by varying the amount of compression. Introduce the geometry of an ellipse and the eccentricity equation.

Setup around the room multiple Moon Viewers rings with varying amounts of eccentricity. (Tie string from side to side to hold desired shape). Tape ring to lab table and designate the foci points inside the ring. Using a meter stick, have small groups of students compute the eccentricity of the orbit at each station around the room.

Students Get it

The Moon Viewer simplifies difficult topics by reducing student confusion. Other models have students walk around the observer while trying to hold a side of a tiny moon on a stick facing one direction. This rarely works. The Moon Viewer eliminates this error by attaching moons on a large ring in the correct orientation and keeping them in place while students observe them from polar and earthbound views.

Eight Moons for Eight Phases

A complete model. Included is a large 36” ring with eight mounting points, one for each moon and therefore each moon phase. Moons are painted half white and half black with matching attachment points. When oriented correctly, it illustrates all eight phases. Students can refer back to the model to complete their drawings. No extra add-ons needed.

Inquiry Based and Hands-On

Direct manipulation by students. With the Moonviewer, students can explore different arrangements of the moons to test their own theories of why we observe phases of the moon. Afterwards groups can present and demonstrate their theories to the class. Or, students can follow the traditional “cookbook” lab usually found with other models and complete hand-outs. Once you have the Moonviewer you’ll discover what works best for you and your students.
